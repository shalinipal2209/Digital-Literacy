PART A: CASE STUDY ON UPI/ONLINE PAYMENT FRAUD

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) fraud is a rapidly growing cybercrime where 
attackers trick users into authorizing fraudulent transactions. It typically begins 
with the fraudster contacting the victim via phone, SMS, or messaging apps, often 
pretending to be a bank representative, delivery agent, or buyer. 
A common step-by-step method starts with the attacker gaining trust by offering 
a believable story—such as confirming a refund or payment. Next, the victim is 
asked to “accept” a payment request or scan a QR code. In reality, these actions 
authorize money to be sent from the victim’s account. Sometimes, the fraudster 
persuades the victim to share sensitive details like OTPs or UPI PINs, claiming it is 
required to complete a transaction. Once access is granted, the money is quickly 
transferred to multiple accounts, making recovery difficult. 
Targets are usually everyday smartphone users, especially those unfamiliar with 
digital payment security—such as elderly individuals, small business owners, or 
first-time UPI users. 
The consequences can be severe. Victims may lose significant amounts of money 
within minutes, often with little chance of recovery. Beyond financial loss, such 
incidents cause emotional distress, loss of trust in digital systems, and 
reputational damage in some cases. This type of fraud highlights the importance 
of digital awareness, as most scams rely on social engineering rather than 
technical hacking. 

PUBLISHED INCIDENT: UPI FRAUD VIA INVESTMENT SCAM (INDIA 2026) 
A recent case reported by The Times of India involved a 35-year-old man from 
Ahmedabad who lost money through a UPI fraud scam.  
The victim joined a Telegram group that promised high returns from stock market 
investments. After gaining his trust, the fraudsters asked him to transfer ₹25,000 
to a UPI ID, assuring him that he would receive profits the next day. However, 
after making the payment, he received no response. Later, the scammers 
demanded an additional ₹999 as a “withdrawal fee,” which he also paid. Despite 
this, he never received any returns and realized he had been cheated. 
The victim then reported the incident to the cybercrime helpline and filed a police 
complaint. Authorities began investigating by tracing the UPI transactions and 
mobile numbers used in the fraud.  
This case highlights a common UPI fraud pattern where criminals use social 
engineering and fake investment schemes to trick victims into voluntarily 
transferring money. It also shows how quickly fraudsters disappear after receiving 
payments, making recovery difficult.
